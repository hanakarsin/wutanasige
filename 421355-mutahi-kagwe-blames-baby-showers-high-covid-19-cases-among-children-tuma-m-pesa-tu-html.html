Mutahi Kagwe Blames Baby Showers on High COVID-19 Cases Among Children: &quot;Tuma M-Pesa Tu&quot;

  • Health CS Mutahi Kagwe noted the positive cases of COVID-19 among children were no the rise
  • He pointed at ceremonies like baby showers as the main reason for the rise
  • As of Tuesday, August 3, 57 people aged between zero to nine years had died of COVID-19

Health CS Mutahi Kagwe has pointed at events undertaken by Kenyans as the reason behind the rising cases of COVID-19 among children.

The CS noted that there was laxity on the COVID protocols leading to the current rise in the positivity rate.

Kagwe said events and ceremonies such as baby showers had exposed the young to the virus.

"Gathering for even baby showers have started all over again…This is why we are recording infections in the young ages. Ikiwa mtoto amezaliwa tuma tu M-Pesa (If a baby has been born just sent money via M-Pesa)," he said.

The Ministry of Health said one out of the 25 deaths occurred between Tuesday and Wednesday, while 24 were late death reports on diverse dates between August 2020 and July 2021.

The government statistics as of Tuesday, August 3, showed Kenya had lost 57 people who are aged between zero to nine to COVID-19, those between 10-19 are 31, 20-29 (122), 30-39 (322), 40-49 (472), 50-59 (796), and 60 and above (2,196).

The national COVID-19 positivity rate stood at 15.4% as of Tuesday, August 3, after 1,085 people out of 7,067 samples tested positive.

The cases brought to 2,149,376 the total cumulative tests so far conducted in the country since March 2020, while the number of confirmed positive cases jumped to 205,356.

COVID-19 Vaccination

Kenya has administered 1.7 million vaccines, with the first dose being 1,067,615, while those who received second doses are 666,398.

On July 31, Kenya's capacity to vaccinate its citizens against coronavirus got a boost after the government received an additional 410,000 doses of AstraZeneca vaccine doses.

UK vaccine pledge

The consignment was the first batch of 817,000 COVID-19 vaccine doses the UK Prime Minister Boris Johnson pledged to send to Kenya during President Uhuru Kenyatta's three-day visit.

Half of the doses were to be shipped through a direct bilateral donation, and the remaining half through a UK donation to the COVAX facility.

Kenya also expects to receive 271,440 doses of Pfizer from the COVAX facility and another 1,760,000 doses of Pfizer from the US government.

The government had procured 13 million doses of Johnson and Johnson through the AVAT-AU mechanism, whose delivery will start in August.
